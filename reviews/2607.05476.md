# Review — Parameter-Free Encoders Remain Viable for RDB Foundation Models

- **arXiv:** [2607.05476](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.05476) (v1, cs.LG, Jul 2026)
- **Authors:** Linjie Xu, David Wipf
- **Venue signal:** styled as ICML submission

## Problem

Relational-database (RDB) foundation models aim to predict missing/future values
in arbitrary target columns across unseen databases *without* retraining per task.
A live design dispute frames the paper: do these models need a **parameterized,
pre-trained encoder** (KumoRFM-2, OpenRFM, Relational Transformer), or is a
**parameter-free subgraph encoder** bolted onto a frontier single-table FM
(the RDBLearn / "No need to train your RDB FM" line) enough? The specific
pressure point is whether feeding *observable neighborhood labels* into a
trainable encoder — a standard graph-learning trick (label propagation, masked
label prediction) — tips the balance toward parameterized encoders.

## Method

Two complementary contributions:

1. **Theory (the core).** The paper formalizes two roles labels can play as
   encoder inputs and proves limits on trainable parameters exploiting either
   under the frozen-encoder FM regime:
   - **Prop. 3.1 (labels as discriminative features).** For any label-augmented
     ego-network `G*_H(x_test)` with ≥1 past label, there exist RDB
     distributions where `y_test` is a deterministic function of the *full*
     augmented graph, yet any *fixed* encoder + head does no better than chance
     (KL ≥ log 2). The construction uses a parity rule over neighborhood labels:
     it is identifiable given per-task supervised training (part 2) but *not*
     from the fixed context a foundation model sees (part 1). Heterophily, not
     homophily, is what breaks the fixed encoder.
   - **Prop. 4.1 (labels for feature-importance screening).** Even with
     neighborhood labels supplied, the encoder gains no information about which
     feature columns are the informative support set:
     `p(S | G*_H) = p(S | G_H)`. So labels don't help a dataset-agnostic
     encoder filter uninformative columns.
2. **Empirics.** An updated **RDBLearn v1.1** (parameter-free aggregation
   encoder — adds 25/75 quantiles, categorical entropy, numeric-cast option;
   base predictors extended to TabICL-v2 / TabPFN-v3; default H=4, TabPFN-v2.5
   when no validation set) is run *straight from the open repo with zero RDB
   exposure* against open- and closed-source FMs plus supervised baselines.

## Key results

Across six benchmark suites (RelBench-v1/v2, 4DBInfer, SALT):

- **Best overall among open-source FMs on every suite.**
- Beats **all closed-source FMs on 4 of 6** benchmarks; the only exceptions are
  two RelBench-v1 suites where closed **TabPFN-REL** and (conditionally)
  **KumoRFM-2** edge it out.
- 4DBInfer: avg rank **2.2** (best FM). RelBench-v2 classification: avg AUROC
  **80.07**, rank **1.6**, ahead of KumoRFM-2 (79.78). SALT: avg MRR **0.86**,
  rank **1.38**, beating both KumoRFM variants. RelBench-v2 regression: ties
  RelAgent for best rank (**1.5**).
- Supporting the theory: a trivial homophily "entity mean" predictor (66.7 avg
  AUROC) nearly matches Relational Transformer (69.7), and the `RandGNN`
  ablation (random-weight GNN mixing columns) underperforms RDBLearn.

## Fit with the tabular curriculum

Directly extends the **encoder debate** the curriculum flags in the Y4/Y5
"Universal Row Encoder vs. training-free relational ICL" thread, and it is the
follow-up to Xu et al. 2026 (RDBLearn / "No need to train your RDB FM") already
in play. It reinforces the **honest-baseline** spine (TabReD → TabArena →
BeyondArena): a training-free, reproducible pipeline that any parameterized RDB
FM must clear. It also operationalizes the tabular↔relational seam — a
single-table FM (TabPFN/TabICL) made competitive on relational tasks purely via
parameter-free vertical aggregation — which is exactly the bridge the thesis
lives on. Pairs naturally with the "where ICL breaks" lectures via its
heterophily/parity failure-mode framing.

## Caveats / limitations

- **Theory is existential, not typical.** Both propositions construct adversarial
  parity distributions to show trainable encoders *can't be guaranteed* to win —
  they do **not** show parameter-free is generally better. The authors
  explicitly concede labels may still help parameter-free encoders (and RDBLearn
  doesn't yet even use neighborhood labels).
- **Comparison asymmetry.** Closed-source numbers are taken from other papers'
  reported results with author-tuned, per-task hyperparameters (KumoRFM-2's
  settings are undisclosed / possibly validation-entangled); the paper flags but
  can't fully control for this. RDBLearn also merges train+val on some tasks.
- **Leakage / benchmark-scarcity worries** are acknowledged for LLM-agent
  baselines and public RDB benchmarks generally — extrapolation to siloed
  enterprise data is uncertain (echoes the enterprise-vs-public-benchmark thread).
- RDBLearn **ignores text columns** entirely and lacks label-aware encoding;
  several closed models' enhancements (text handling, ensembling, ICL sampling)
  are unimplemented, so headroom on both sides is unresolved.
- Depends on frontier single-table FMs (TabPFN-v3, TabICL-v2), so its ceiling is
  tied to that external line rather than RDB-native modeling.

## Take-home

Until further advances, a **parameter-free** RDB encoder + frontier single-table
FM stays competitive with the best pre-trained, parameterized industry encoders —
and the label-based arguments for parameterized encoders are, at minimum, not
decisive.
