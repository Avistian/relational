# Review — A Fair Benchmarking of Deep Relational Database Learning Models

- **arXiv:** [2607.03659](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.03659) (v1, cs.DB, Jul 2026)
- **Authors:** Kazi F. Akhter, Bharath Ajendla (SAP AI Research), Manar D. Samad (Tennessee State University)
- **Code:** https://github.com/mdsamad001/Benchmarking-Deep-Relational-Database-Models

## Problem

Deep learning methods for relational databases (RDBs) — GNNs, graph
transformers, and RDB foundation models — have each been published under
*inconsistent* protocols: different epochs/model-selection rules, text/numeric
encoders, neighborhood-sampling strategies, hardware, and cherry-picked
baselines. That makes reported gains impossible to compare and leaves open
whether any of them actually beats a strong single-table model. The paper is a
first-of-its-kind controlled benchmark, deliberately proposing no new method.

## Method

Five RelBench databases (rel-amazon, rel-avito, rel-f1, rel-stack, rel-trial),
each with one classification + one regression task, spanning small (<100k) to
large (>1M) task-row scales. Five learners are refactored into a single
protocol: **LightGBM** and **TabPFN 2.5** (single-table baselines),
**DBFormer** (hybrid graph-transformer, trained per-DB), **Griffin** (graph FM),
and **Relational Transformer / RT** (cell-level transformer FM).

Key harmonizations for fairness:
- **Hop-based neighborhood control.** A hop = one PK–FK step; they evaluate
  hop = 0 (single table), 1, and 2 uniformly, with temporal-leakage guards.
  Non-relational baselines (LightGBM, TabPFN) get multi-hop info via
  RelBench-style flattening/aggregation of connected tables.
- **Common encoders.** MiniLM-v2 (384-d) for all text/categorical encoding
  (replacing each model's native GloVe/Nomic/MiniLM choices) to control cost and
  confound.
- **Common budget/selection.** 100 epochs, best-on-validation model selection,
  embedding size 256, lr 1e-4, batch 256 where applicable.

## Key results

RT is the strongest overall on both task types and every hop setting.

- **Classification (avg rank, lower=better):** RT 1.6/1.2/1.0 at hop 0/1/2;
  Griffin second (3.6/2.2/2.4); TabPFN 2.5 mid-pack (3.2/3.0/…); DBFormer weakest.
  Pairwise wins (of 15 cases): RT beats LightGBM 14/15, TabPFN 13/15, Griffin 14/15.
- **Regression (avg rank):** RT best (2.0/2.4/2.0); TabPFN 2.5 competitive at
  hop 1 (ties RT at 2.4) and beats RT on rel-stack post-votes at all hops
  (likely prior-distribution overlap from synthetic pretraining).
- **Deep RDB methods beat TabPFN 2.5 even at hop=0 (single table):** RT ranks
  1.6 vs TabPFN's 4th in classification — attributed to RDB tasks having far
  more samples than TabPFN's <10k sweet spot.
- **Diminishing returns past hop 1.** hop=2 rarely beats hop=1 (e.g. Griffin
  RMSE unchanged on rel-avito/rel-f1), while cost roughly doubles (Griffin on
  rel-f1 driver-dnf: 202→403 min for ~2% AUC). rel-trial classification even
  regresses at hop=2 for all but RT.

## Fit with the tabular curriculum

This is the direct RDB-side extension of the **honest-baseline / fair-comparison
spine** (TabReD → TabArena → BeyondArena): a protocol-consistent benchmark that
any relational-DL claim must now clear, and a baseline suite the thesis will be
measured against. It supplies concrete evidence for two curriculum threads:
(1) the **tabular↔relational seam** — that multi-table structure adds real
signal over single-table TabPFN when sample counts are large — and (2) the
**"where TabPFN v2/2.5 breaks"** critical lectures, here from the scale/relational
angle (large N and PK–FK dependence, not just distribution shift). The
hop-1-vs-hop-2 diminishing-returns finding is a useful, citable rule of thumb.

## Caveats / limitations

- **Tiny grid, high variance.** Only five DBs / one task each per type; several
  ranking swaps sit inside plausible noise (authors flag the rel-avito hop-1
  TabPFN>RT gap as likely variance). No repeated seeds / error bars reported.
- **Enforced-encoder confound.** Swapping every model to MiniLM-v2 controls cost
  but *handicaps* Griffin/RT, which were designed around Nomic/their own encoders
  — "fair" here also means "not each model at its best."
- **Hardware asymmetry.** Relational models run on a DGX Spark GB10; baselines on
  an i9/RTX-4090 workstation, so the timing comparisons are only loosely
  commensurable.
- **RelBench-only + leakage risk.** All data is public RelBench; extrapolation to
  siloed enterprise RDBs (the paper's own motivation) is untested, and the
  aggregation-based flattening for LightGBM/TabPFN may under- or over-represent
  neighbor signal vs. the native relational models.
- **RT's edge partly a capacity/compute story**, not isolated architecture — the
  study characterizes rather than explains *why* transformers beat GNNs here.

## Take-home

Under a single honest protocol, the **Relational Transformer wins** and deep RDB
models beat TabPFN 2.5 even on single tables at RDB scale — but the payoff from
going beyond 1-hop neighborhoods is usually not worth its compute. A benchmark
to cite, with the usual small-grid caveats.
