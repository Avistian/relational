# Review — Exploring Differences Between Tabular Enterprise Data and Public Benchmarks

- **arXiv:** [2606.30452](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.30452) (v1, cs.LG, Jun 2026)
- **Authors:** Myung Jun Kim, Maximilian Schambach, Frank Essenberger, Andre Sres, Johannes Höhne (SAP)

## Problem

Tabular ML research is driven by **public benchmarks**, yet **enterprise tables** —
the backbone of real business operations — are almost never characterized. If
enterprise data differs statistically from public benchmarks, then leaderboard
rankings may not transfer to deployment. The paper asks: *how different is
enterprise data, and does benchmark performance predict enterprise performance?*

## Method

- **Data-statistics analysis** contrasting enterprise tables with standard tabular
  benchmarks (distributional characteristics of real business data).
- **Performance measurements** of modern tabular foundation models — **TabPFN,
  TabICL, ConTextTab** — on both benchmark and enterprise data, to test whether
  benchmark strength carries over.

## Key results

- Enterprise data **markedly differs** from public tabular benchmarks in its
  statistics.
- A model that is **strong on typical benchmarks can perform poorly on real
  enterprise data — and vice versa**: benchmark leadership does not generalize.
- Conclusion: the field needs **additional benchmarks with enterprise-grade
  characteristics** to be representative of business use.

## Fit with the tabular curriculum

Reinforces the **honest-baseline / distribution-realism spine**
(TabReD → TabArena → BeyondArena): a concrete demonstration that "SOTA on public
benchmarks" ≠ "works in deployment," now specifically for **TFMs** (TabPFN/TabICL/
ConTextTab). It is the deployment-gap counterpart to TabReD's temporal-split
critique and pairs tightly with the FlexTab and TabPack papers (same enterprise
concern, same SAP group / TabReD-heavy evaluations). Good grounding for a critical
"where do TFM benchmarks mislead us" lecture, and a caution to attach to any TFM
result the curriculum presents.

## Caveats / limitations

- **Enterprise data is proprietary** — the analysis is likely not fully
  reproducible and the specific datasets can't be shared, limiting external
  verification and re-use as a public benchmark (which the paper itself calls for
  but does not yet deliver).
- Largely **diagnostic**: documents a gap and its direction, but offers no method
  to close it (no adaptation recipe, no released enterprise benchmark).
- Model set is a snapshot (TabPFN/TabICL/ConTextTab); conclusions may shift with
  newer TFMs.
- "Differs statistically" needs the paper's specifics (which statistics, how much)
  to be actionable — not captured at abstract level here.

## Take-home

Enterprise tables are statistically unlike public benchmarks, and TFM benchmark
wins do not reliably transfer to them — a pointed reminder for the curriculum's
honest-baseline thread that leaderboard success and deployment success can diverge,
and that enterprise-grade benchmarks are still missing.
