Metrics: ROC vs PR & Calibration

Lesson 008 · Phase 1 · ~40 min

Lesson 007 told you to stop trusting accuracy and lead with PR-AUC. This lesson makes that precise, and then separates two things almost everyone conflates: how well a model ranks (ROC, PR) versus whether its probabilities can be believed (calibration). A model can ace the first and fail the second — and for a deployed risk score, the second is what bites you.

This lesson's single skill: choose and read the right evaluation curve for the question, and check (then fix) calibration with a reliability curve and the Brier score.

Two curves, two questions

CurveAxesAnswersNo-skill baseline
ROCTPR vs FPRhow well does it rank positives above negatives?0.5, always
PRPrecision vs Recallat a given recall, how clean are the positive calls?prevalence (the positive rate)

The baseline column is the whole point. A useless model scores ROC-AUC 0.5 no matter how rare the positives are — but its PR-AUC floor is the prevalence. So on a 2% positive problem, "PR-AUC 0.30" is real skill (15× the baseline) while "ROC-AUC 0.80" might be mediocre. ROC's false-positive-rate denominator is dominated by the huge negative class, so it stays optimistic when negatives swamp positives (Saito & Rehmsmeier 2015).

Verified (sklearn 1.9, 17.7% positive, RandomForest): ROC-AUC 0.863 vs PR-AUC 0.720, with a PR no-skill baseline of 0.177. Same model, same predictions — ROC just reads higher because its denominator is rich in easy true negatives. Report the PR/average-precision number on rare-positive tasks, and always state the prevalence so the baseline is visible.

Use ROC-AUC when you care about ranking quality across the whole range and classes are roughly balanced; use PR-AUC / average precision (and MAP for ranking tasks) when positives are rare and you act on the top of the ranked list. They can even disagree on which model is better (Davis & Goadrich 2006) — pick the one that matches how the model will be used.

Ranking is not the same as trustworthy probabilities

AUROC and AUPRC only care about order. A model that outputs 0.51 for every positive and 0.49 for every negative ranks perfectly and has AUROC 1.0 — yet none of those numbers are real probabilities. The moment you act on the probability — threshold a fraud score, multiply a churn probability by customer value, feed risk into a downstream decision — you need calibration: when the model says 0.7, the event should happen about 70% of the time.

The reliability curve

To check calibration, bin predictions and plot the mean predicted probability against the observed frequency in each bin. Perfect calibration lies on the diagonal. Toggle a raw model vs its calibrated version:

This is the textbook distortion of an out-of-the-box RandomForest: variance in the averaged trees pushes predictions away from 0 and 1, so the curve bows off the diagonal — at a predicted 0.55 the event actually occurs ~72% of the time (Niculescu-Mizil & Caruana 2005). Boosted trees and SVMs show the same sigmoid; logistic regression is usually close to calibrated already.

One number for calibration: the Brier score

The Brier score is the mean squared error between predicted probability and the 0/1 outcome — lower is better. It is a proper scoring rule: it rewards probabilities that are both well-ranked and well-calibrated, so it's the single number to track when probabilities matter.

RandomForest probabilitiesBrier (↓)
raw0.0898
Platt / sigmoid calibration0.0856
isotonic calibration0.0853
Verified. Calibrating the same forest lowered Brier from 0.0898 to 0.0853 without retraining the ranker — AUROC is unchanged, the probabilities just moved onto the diagonal. Small absolute numbers (most cases are easy negatives), but the mid-range is exactly where decisions live.

The fix: CalibratedClassifierCV

Two standard mappings turn scores into calibrated probabilities: sigmoid (Platt scaling) — fit a 1-parameter logistic, great for the sigmoid distortion of trees/SVMs and robust on small data; and isotonic — fit a monotonic step function, more flexible but needs more data or it overfits. 

from sklearn.calibration import CalibratedClassifierCV, calibration_curve
from sklearn.metrics import brier_score_loss

cal = CalibratedClassifierCV(RandomForestClassifier(), method="sigmoid", cv=5)
cal.fit(X_train, y_train)                       # calibrates on held-out inner folds
p = cal.predict_proba(X_test)[:, 1]

brier = brier_score_loss(y_test, p)
frac_pos, mean_pred = calibration_curve(y_test, p, n_bins=10)   # the reliability curve

The cv argument matters: CalibratedClassifierCV fits the calibrator on data the base model didn't train on, the same leak-free instinct as Lesson 005. Calibrate on the training set via internal CV, then report Brier and the reliability curve on the untouched test set.

Bridge to the thesis

Metrics are how the thesis will be won or lost on paper. Three consequences:

  • Match the metric to the RelBench task. Entity classification (churn, fraud) and recommendation/link prediction are rare-positive and top-of-list — report AUPRC / MAP with the prevalence stated, not accuracy or bare AUROC. The leaderboards do this for a reason.
  • A better AUROC is not automatically a better model. If your RDL model wins on AUROC but the baseline wins on AUPRC at the operating recall, say so — that's the honest-limitations posture the mission demands.
  • Deployed relational scores must be calibrated. A churn probability that feeds an expected-value calculation, or a fraud score with a cost threshold, is useless if 0.7 doesn't mean 70%. GBDTs and GNNs are both frequently mis-calibrated; calibrating and reporting Brier is part of a baseline a skeptic will accept.

Four mistakes to catch

  1. Reporting AUROC alone on rare positives. It hides the prevalence baseline. Fix: lead with AUPRC/MAP and state the positive rate.
  2. Treating ranking scores as probabilities. AUROC says nothing about calibration. Fix: reliability curve + Brier.
  3. Calibrating on the test set. Leaks the answer. Fix: CalibratedClassifierCV(cv=...) on train, evaluate on test.
  4. Isotonic on tiny data. Overfits the calibration map. Fix: prefer sigmoid when data is limited.

Check your understanding

Primary reading (today)

Read Saito & Rehmsmeier, The Precision-Recall Plot Is More Informative than the ROC Plot… (PLOS ONE 2015) — focus on why ROC misleads under imbalance and what the PR baseline is. Then skim scikit-learn's Probability calibration guide (§1.16) for reliability curves, sigmoid vs isotonic, and the Brier score. The deeper calibration reference is Niculescu-Mizil & Caruana 2005.
