XGBoost

Lesson 014 · Phase 1 · ~45 min

Lesson 013 built gradient boosting by hand: fit a tree to the residuals, add a shrunken slice, repeat. XGBoost (Chen & Guestrin, 2016) is that idea made regularized, exact, and fast — and for a decade it has been the model to beat on tabular data. This lesson's single skill: explain XGBoost's regularized objective and its structure-score split gain, and know which knobs (λ, γ, learning rate, subsampling) control over/underfitting — then tune it on a real task.

One sentence. XGBoost keeps L013's stagewise recipe but (1) uses a second-order (gradient and curvature) approximation of the loss, and (2) adds an explicit regularizer on tree complexity, which yields a closed-form best leaf weight and a principled split-gain that can prune itself.

From residuals to a regularized objective

Plain gradient boosting fits the negative gradient. XGBoost writes down what it is really minimizing at round t and keeps the second-order term of the Taylor expansion. For each row it needs two numbers: the gradient gᵢ (slope of the loss) and the hessian hᵢ (curvature). It also adds a penalty on the tree itself:

Ω(f) = γ·T + ½·λ·Σⱼ wⱼ²

where T is the number of leaves, wⱼ the weight (output value) of leaf j, γ a flat cost per leaf, and λ an L2 penalty that pulls leaf weights toward zero. Regularization is no longer a side-effect of shrinkage — it is written into the objective.

The two formulas that fall out

Because the objective is now a tidy quadratic in each leaf's weight, the best weight has a closed form. Let Gⱼ = Σ gᵢ and Hⱼ = Σ hᵢ be the sums of gradients and hessians of the rows landing in leaf j:

wⱼ* = − Gⱼ / (Hⱼ + λ)

Plugging that back in, a fixed tree structure has a structure score (lower is better) whose per-leaf term Gⱼ²/(Hⱼ+λ) measures how useful that leaf is. Deciding whether to split a node is then just: does splitting improve the score more than it costs?

Gain = ½ [ GL²/(HL+λ) + GR²/(HR+λ) − (GL+GR)²/(HL+HR+λ) ] − γ
Callback — Lesson 011. Recognize the shape? This is exactly the ΔG = children − parent logic from decision-tree splits, just measured in G²/(H+λ) instead of Gini. The bracket is left child quality + right child quality − parent quality; the −γ is a toll. If the children don't beat the parent by more than γ, the gain is negative and XGBoost doesn't make the split. That is built-in pruning.

Feel the regularizers

A candidate split sends fixed gradient/hessian statistics into a left and right leaf. Drag λ and watch every leaf weight and every score shrink toward zero — that is L2 regularization. Drag γ up: it lifts a red toll line, and once γ exceeds the raw gain the verdict flips to PRUNED. These are the same two numbers the lab computes by hand and checks against XGBoost's own leaf outputs.

Why XGBoost actually won (it isn't just the math)

The regularized objective is elegant, but XGBoost's dominance came as much from systems and algorithmic engineering. Four pieces matter for you:

  • Sparsity-aware split finding. Every split learns a default direction for missing values from the data itself — a direct, learned answer to the missingness problem of Lesson 006. No imputation required; missing is a first-class citizen.
  • Weighted quantile sketch. Instead of testing every threshold, it proposes candidate split points from a hessian-weighted histogram — this is what makes it scale to millions of rows (and it's what sklearn's HistGradientBoosting and LightGBM also do).
  • Column & row subsampling. Borrowed from Random Forests (L012): sample features/rows per tree to decorrelate and regularize.
  • Shrinkage (learning rate). The same η knob from L013 — small steps, many trees.

Two honest lessons here. First, on 1,000 noisy rows XGBoost's out-of-the-box edge over sklearn is small — the Random Forest's 0.901 from L012 is still competitive. The win comes from tuning (+0.017), and tuning is a cost you must pay honestly. Second, the tuned config leans on regularization (λ=10, shallow trees, row subsampling) precisely because the data is small and noisy — exactly what the formulas above predict.

Thesis bridge. A properly tuned XGBoost is the single-table baseline the relational thesis must beat fairly (Grinsztajn 2022; the whole point of Year 1). "We beat a default XGBoost" is not a result — defaults were nearly matched by 2016-era sklearn. When a relational model later wins, it must win against a tuned booster, and the win has to come from structure the GBDT cannot see across joins, not from a lazy baseline.

Where XGBoost breaks (know this too)

  • Needs tuning to shine; untuned it's only a hair above sklearn's GBDT (see table).
  • No free lunch on tiny, noisy data — a Random Forest can tie or win (L012/L013).
  • Can overfit without λ/γ/early-stopping, especially deep trees.
  • Still a single-table model: it never sees relational structure you didn't hand-engineer.
