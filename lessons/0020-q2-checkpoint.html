Q2 Checkpoint: Match a Published Tree Baseline

Lesson 020 · Phase 1 · ~45 min · capstone

Q2 taught you how tree ensembles work — splits, bagging, boosting, XGBoost, LightGBM, CatBoost, tuning, stacking — and why they win. This checkpoint is not a new concept: it is the moment you prove you can reproduce a published tree baseline honestly and then try to beat it. Everything the thesis argues after this — that relational models unlock value the flat table misses — is measured against exactly this kind of baseline, built exactly this carefully.

This checkpoint's single skill: reproduce a published-style tree baseline under a fair, fixed protocol — same data, same split, same metric, a disclosed tuning budget — then match or beat it with a tuned XGBoost / LightGBM and a leak-free stacked ensemble, reporting the result honestly.

The fair-comparison contract

"Match or beat a published number" is meaningless unless the comparison is fair. A reproduction you can defend fixes five things before touching a model, and reports the sixth:

  • Same dataset. The exact rows and target the reference used (here: OpenML adult, income > 50K).
  • Same split. One fixed, stratified holdout with a pinned seed — the test set is touched once, at the very end.
  • Same metric. One headline number chosen up front (ROC-AUC here), with prevalence stated so it is readable.
  • Disclosed tuning budget. Tuning is CV on train only, with a stated number of trials — not "I searched until I won."
  • Same preprocessing scope. Every fit-bearing transform inside the pipeline, so no fold sees the others.
  • Reported honestly. The verdict (matched / beat / failed) and the size of the gap, not a cherry-picked run.
Why this is the checkpoint. The single most common way a "relational model beats the baseline" claim collapses is an unfair baseline — undertuned, leaky, or scored on a different split. Q3 (evaluation rigor) is built on this contract; the whole thesis lives or dies by it. Learn to be the reviewer who would reject your own sloppy comparison.

The reference baseline

On adult (48,842 rows, ~24% positive), a competently-configured gradient-boosted tree is a commonly reported baseline at roughly ROC-AUC 0.92–0.93 and ~87% accuracy. We pre-register the target as a fixed-default XGBoost — no tuning, disclosed hyper-parameters — so it is a number anyone can reproduce. Here is the verified bake-off under the contract above:

Read the honest half: tuning barely moved it

A 15-trial randomized search bought +0.0012 ROC-AUC over an untuned default, and accuracy did not move at all (0.8733 → 0.8734). This is the truth about strong tabular baselines that beginners miss: a sensible default GBDT is already almost the whole story, and heroic tuning is a small polish, not a savior. The discipline that matters here is not "search harder" — it is reproducing the number under a protocol you would defend to a skeptic. If your tuned model beats a default by a lot, suspect a leak or an unfair reference before you celebrate.

Two GBDTs are redundant — stacking confirms the diversity rule

The stacked ensemble gained essentially nothing over the best single model (+0.0001). Look at why: the out-of-fold predictions of the tuned XGBoost and tuned LightGBM correlate at 0.997. They are two leaf-wise/level-wise flavours of the same idea, so they make the same mistakes — and Lesson 018 told you diversity is the fuel of stacking. Stacking two near-identical learners is stacking one. To move this number you would need a genuinely different model class — which, on flat tabular data, is hard to find, and is exactly the opening the relational thesis probes.

Thesis bridge. You have now built the incumbent, honestly and at full strength: a tuned, leak-free, reproducible tree baseline that pins ROC-AUC 0.93 on a real table, where tuning adds a thousandth and a second GBDT adds nothing. That is the bar. The RDL bet is not "trees are weak" — it is that the flat adult table discards relational structure (who a person's employer is, which household they share, the sequence of their jobs) that a model over the original database could exploit. Year 1 closes by naming what flattening throws away; Years 3–4 try to beat this exact baseline by keeping it.

Primary reading (today)

The two checkpoint papers, revisited as the systems you are reproducing:

  • Chen & Guestrin (2016), XGBoost: A Scalable Tree Boosting System (KDD) — arXiv:1603.02754. Re-read §2 (the regularized objective) and skim §6 (the end-to-end evaluation) to see how the authors report a baseline fairly.
  • Ke et al. (2017), LightGBM: A Highly Efficient Gradient Boosting Decision Tree (NeurIPS) — NeurIPS 2017. Note the experimental protocol in §4: same data, same metric, stated budget — the contract you are practising.
