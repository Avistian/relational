Missingness: MCAR / MAR / MNAR

Lesson 006 · Phase 1 · ~40 min

Lesson 005 taught you to put every imputer inside a Pipeline so it refits per fold. That answers where to impute. This lesson answers the harder question your last lesson quietly skipped: why are the values missing in the first place — and why the answer decides whether your imputation is harmless or quietly poisons the result.

This lesson's single skill: given a hole in your data, classify its mechanism as MCAR, MAR, or MNAR by asking one question — what does the probability of being missing depend on? — and then pick a handling that does not bias your baseline.

One question, three answers

Donald Rubin's 1976 taxonomy splits all missingness by what the missingness depends on. Let R mark which cells are missing. Then (van Buuren, FIMD §2.2.4):

MechanismProbability of missing depends on…Plain words
MCAR
completely at random		nothing (just a constant)The gaps are pure chance. The observed rows are a fair random sample.
MAR
at random		only observed dataThe gaps depend on columns you can see. Conditioning on them removes the bias.
MNAR
not at random		the unobserved value itselfThe gaps depend on the very value that's missing. Nothing observed can fully explain them.
The names mislead everyone once. "MAR" is not "missing randomly" — that's MCAR. MAR means: random once you condition on what you observed. The ladder is "depends on nothing → depends on the observed → depends on the unobserved."

Van Buuren's weighing scale

The cleanest intuition, straight from the primary source, is a scale that sometimes fails to record a weight:

  • MCAR: the scale randomly drops readings — a loose wire. Missingness is unrelated to anything.
  • MAR: the scale drops more readings on a soft surface than a hard one. If you recorded the surface, the missingness depends only on something observed.
  • MNAR: the scale wears out and drops more readings for heavier objects — and you never noticed. Missingness depends on the weight itself, the thing you failed to measure.

Under MAR the gaps line up with the ringed high-X2 rows — a column you can see. Under MNAR the gaps are exactly the high-X1 rows (ghosted): the missing value caused its own absence, and X2 gives you no warning.

Why the label decides your method

The mechanism is not academic — it dictates which "fixes" are safe. Take the two reflexes most people reach for:

HandlingUnbiased under…Breaks under…
Drop incomplete rows (complete-case / listwise)MCAR onlyMAR, MNAR — you delete a non-random slice and skew every estimate
Mean / median imputationnothing, reallyall — it shrinks variance and flattens relationships even under MCAR
Conditional imputation (regress on observed cols, e.g. IterativeImputer)MCAR, MARMNAR — the predictor it needs is itself missing
Add a missing-indicator columnkeeps MNAR signal usabledoesn't recover the value, but lets the model use "was-missing" as a feature
The trap. Complete-case deletion feels safe — you only "use real data." But under MAR/MNAR the rows you drop differ systematically from the rows you keep, so every downstream number (a mean, a coefficient, a CV score) is biased. Deleting data is a modelling decision, not a neutral cleanup.

You cannot fully test which one you have

You can test MCAR-vs-not (e.g. compare observed-data distributions across the missing/observed split), but MAR vs MNAR is untestable from the data alone — the information you'd need is exactly what's missing (FIMD §2.2.4). So the mechanism is a domain judgement: you argue it from how the data was generated. That is why naming it explicitly, and citing your reasoning, is part of an honest baseline.

The tools (all inside the Pipeline from Lesson 005)

sklearn's §6.4 imputation gives you three building blocks. Every one of them has a fit, so — by the rule you just learned — every one lives inside the pipeline and refits per fold. 

from sklearn.impute import SimpleImputer, MissingIndicator
from sklearn.experimental import enable_iterative_imputer  # noqa: required first
from sklearn.impute import IterativeImputer

# 1) Simple + keep the "was it missing?" signal (crucial under MNAR)
imp = SimpleImputer(strategy="median", add_indicator=True)

# 2) Conditional imputation — models each column from the others (MAR-friendly)
imp = IterativeImputer(max_iter=10, random_state=0)

add_indicator=True appends a 0/1 column per feature that had missing values: the model can now learn that "this was missing" is itself predictive — the only way to keep MNAR information once you've filled the hole. Note IterativeImputer is still experimental, so the enable_iterative_imputer import must come first. 

from sklearn.pipeline import Pipeline
from sklearn.linear_model import LogisticRegression

pipe = Pipeline([
    ("impute", SimpleImputer(strategy="median", add_indicator=True)),
    ("clf",    LogisticRegression(max_iter=1000)),
])
# refit per fold, indicator and all — no leakage, MNAR signal preserved

Bridge to the thesis

Relational data is saturated with missingness, and it is rarely MCAR. A customer with no orders, a product with no reviews, a null foreign key — these gaps usually depend on the very thing you want to predict (a churned user stops generating rows). That is structural, MNAR-flavoured missingness, the same idea you flagged back in Lesson 002.

This is exactly where the paradigms diverge, and where your fair baseline lives:

  • GBDTs (your XGBoost baseline) handle NaN natively: each split learns a default direction for missing values, effectively using missingness as a free feature. A fair tabular baseline leans on this — don't impute away a signal the tree could have used.
  • Relational deep learning represents "missing" structurally: a missing relation is simply an absent edge / absent neighbour in the entity graph. The model never sees a NaN; it sees "no message arrived." The thesis bet is that this structural encoding captures informative missingness more faithfully than any hand-built indicator column.

So to argue RDL is undervalued, your manual baseline must treat missingness deliberately — name the mechanism, keep the indicator, never silently drop rows — or a reviewer will say you handicapped the baseline. Flawless missing-data discipline is, again, the bar the thesis must clear.

Four mistakes to catch

  1. Calling MAR "random." MAR depends on observed columns; MCAR is the random one. Mislabel and you'll trust complete-case analysis when you shouldn't.
  2. Dropping incomplete rows by default. Unbiased only under MCAR. Fix: impute inside the pipeline; keep the rows.
  3. Mean-imputing and moving on. Destroys variance and hides MNAR signal. Fix: add_indicator=True, or let trees handle NaN natively.
  4. Imputing on the full table before CV. The Lesson 005 leak. Fix: imputer is a pipeline step, refit per fold.
