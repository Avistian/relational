Embeddings for Categoricals

Lesson 031 · Phase 1 · Q4 · ~55 min · core paper: Guo & Berkhahn 2016, Entity Embeddings

Welcome to Q4. Q3 closed with a hard, honest verdict: inside the single-table world, cleverer search (tuning, AutoML, L029) and cleverer architecture (the neural baselines of L028) are nearly out of road — everything ties a well-tuned GBDT (L030). If the remaining upside is not in search or architecture, it must be in the representation — how the raw data is turned into the numbers a model sees. Q4 is the bridge to that idea, and it starts at the most everyday place a representation is chosen: how do you turn a categorical column into numbers?

This is not a small question. Real databases are full of categoricals — product ids, zip codes, merchant names, diagnosis codes — often with thousands or millions of levels. The way you encode them decides how much signal survives, whether you leak the label, and how much memory you burn. And one of the four encodings we meet today — the entity embedding — is your first taste of a learned representation, the exact seed that grows into the relational deep learning the thesis is betting on.

Its single skill: know the four ways to encode a categorical for a model — one-hot, ordinal, target (mean) encoding, and learned entity embeddings — why one-hot is safe-but-wide, why ordinal invents a false order, why target encoding is powerful but leaks unless done out-of-fold, and how entity embeddings (Guo & Berkhahn 2016) turn each level into a learned dense vector that captures similarity; and understand that on a small single table they all roughly tie, so the embedding's real payoff — compactness at high cardinality and a reusable learned geometry — is a representational one, which is the bridge to neural tabular and RDL.

One sentence. A model eats numbers, so every categorical must be encoded; one-hot is safe but wide, ordinal fakes an order, target encoding is compact and strong but leaks the label unless computed out-of-fold, and entity embeddings learn a dense vector per level that places similar levels close together — the first learned representation, even though on a 1000-row table it only ties the simpler encodings.

First, the vocabulary (nothing here is assumed)

This lesson names several encoding ideas. Each is defined here, in plain language, before we rely on it. Where an idea is a callback to an earlier lesson, the definition is restated inline — do not assume you must remember it.

Categorical variable. A column whose values are labels from a finite set, not quantities: purpose ∈ {new car, education, …}, checking_status ∈ {<0, 0–200, …}. There is no built-in arithmetic — "education minus new car" is meaningless — so a model that only does arithmetic on numbers cannot consume it directly.
Cardinality. The number of distinct levels a categorical has. checking_status has cardinality 4; a zip_code or product_id can have tens of thousands. High cardinality is what makes the choice of encoding matter.
One-hot encoding. Replace a level with a vector of 0s and a single 1: a categorical of cardinality k becomes k binary columns (is_new_car, is_education, …). It assumes nothing false — every level is equally distant from every other — but the design matrix grows by k columns per categorical, and it encodes no notion that two levels might be similar.
Ordinal (label) encoding. Replace each level with an integer (<0→0, 0–200→1, …): one compact column. The catch: integers carry an order and spacing, so this silently asserts <0 < 0–200 < ≥200 < none with equal gaps — a false order when none exists. A linear model must honour that fiction; a tree can split around it (we will measure exactly this).
Target (mean) encoding. Replace each level with the mean of the target for rows of that level: enc(purpose) = P(good | purpose). One dense column, ordered by risk, that scales to any cardinality (Micci-Barreca 2001). Its power is also its danger: it is computed from the label, so done carelessly it leaks (a whole section below).
Smoothing (shrinkage). A rare level's raw target mean is noisy — one lucky row and its "risk" is 1.0. Smoothing pulls each level's estimate toward the global mean by its count: enc(L) = (n_L · mean_L + m · global) / (n_L + m) where n_L is the level's row count and m a smoothing strength (we use m=20). A level with thousands of rows keeps its own mean; a level with three rows falls back to the prior.
Out-of-fold (OOF) encoding. The leak-free recipe for target encoding: split the training rows into folds and encode each fold using level means computed only from the other folds — never from the row being encoded. It is the same "fit on data you don't score" discipline as cross-validation (L003) and leakage-safe pipelines (L005), applied to a feature.
Entity embedding. Replace each level with a short learned dense vector (e.g. 5 or 50 numbers), initialised randomly and trained by backpropagation jointly with the model (Guo & Berkhahn 2016). Because it is learned to help predict the target, levels that behave alike end up near each other in the vector space — a similarity structure no other encoding has. Target encoding is the special 1-dimensional case.
Representation. The numeric form the raw data takes before the model's final decision layer. One-hot, ordinal, target, and embedding are four representations of the same column. Q3 showed search and architecture are exhausted on single tables; the representation is the axis still open — the whole point of this quarter.

The problem: a model eats numbers, not words

Gradient boosting, logistic regression, neural nets — all of them ultimately do arithmetic on numeric vectors. A column like purpose = "education" has to become numbers first, and how you do that conversion is a modelling decision with real consequences. Here is the same credit_g column, checking_status (4 levels), under all four encodings. Toggle between them and read what each one costs and captures.

Walk the four. One-hot is honest — it asserts nothing false — but a categorical of cardinality k costs k columns (across credit_g's 13 categoricals that is +54 columns; a single high-cardinality id could add thousands). Ordinal is one column, but it fabricates an order and equal spacing. Target is one column and orders levels by their actual risk — but it was built from the label. Embedding is a few columns of learned numbers that can place <0 and 0–200 near each other if the data says they behave alike.

Read it row by row and the whole story of encoding falls out:

  • Ordinal wrecks the linear model but not the tree. Switching one-hot → ordinal drops the linear model from 0.782 to 0.739 — a real fall, because the linear model is forced to treat the fabricated integer order as meaningful (it can only add a constant per unit of the code). The GBDT barely moves (0.778 → 0.774): axis-aligned splits can carve the integer axis back into the original groups, so a false order costs a tree almost nothing. This is why libraries like LightGBM/CatBoost happily take integer-coded categoricals while a linear or neural model would choke on them.
  • Target encoding matches one-hot at a third of the width. Out-of-fold target encoding scores 0.784 (linear) and 0.769 (GBDT) using 20 columns instead of 61. On credit_g the accuracy is a tie; the win is compactness — and that win becomes decisive when a categorical has 10,000 levels and one-hot is simply infeasible.
  • There is no free lunch across models. The best encoding depends on the model: linear models love one-hot or target encoding and hate ordinal; trees tolerate all three. The encoding and the model are a pair.

The pitfall: target encoding leaks the label

Target encoding's strength — it is built from the label — is exactly what makes it dangerous. If you compute a level's mean using the very row you are about to encode (or, worse, over the whole dataset before you split off a test set), you smuggle each row's own answer into its features. The model then "learns" to read the label it was supposed to predict.

Predict first

Imagine a customer_id column where almost every id appears once. We target-encode it two ways — naively (each id's mean over all its rows, computed before the split) and out-of-fold — and feed that column alone to a GBDT on credit_g-sized data. The id has no real predictive content. What test AUC will each produce?

The naive encoding of a near-unique id is a mirror: for an id that appears once, its "mean target" is just its own label, so the feature is the answer. Fed to a GBDT, that content-free column scores a train AUC of 0.891 — and because the encoding was built before the split, even the test AUC reads 0.891. A model that looks brilliant in your report and collapses to chance in production. The out-of-fold version encodes each row from other folds only, so a unique id falls back to the global prior (0.70) and carries no information: train AUC 0.514, test AUC 0.504 — chance, which is the truth.

The fix is a discipline you already know from L002 and L022, now applied to a feature:

  1. Compute the encoding out-of-fold (or with a leave-one-out mean) so a row is never encoded using its own label.
  2. Smooth toward the global mean by count, so rare levels don't memorise a handful of rows: enc(L) = (n_L·mean_L + m·global)/(n_L + m).
  3. Fit the encoder inside the CV fold, exactly like any other learned preprocessing step (L005) — the level means are parameters learned from training data only.

The danger scales with cardinality: a level with thousands of rows barely notices one row's label; a near-unique id is pure leak. That is precisely why the highest-cardinality columns — the ids that point into other tables — are the ones target encoding handles most treacherously, a thread we pick up when we leave the single table behind.

Entity embeddings: a learned representation

Target encoding gave each level a single learned number. Entity embeddings (Guo & Berkhahn 2016) generalise that to a learned vector. Each level gets a row in an embedding table — say d = 5 numbers — initialised at random. The vectors are fed into a neural network and trained by backpropagation along with the rest of the weights, so gradient descent discovers coordinates that help predict the target. A common rule of thumb for the width (from the paper and the fast.ai library) is d = min(50, (cardinality + 1) // 2) so a 4-level column gets a 2-D embedding and a 10,000-level column gets a 50-D one — far narrower than one-hot's 10,000 columns.

Why does this matter? Because a vector can encode similarity. Two levels that affect the target in the same way can be pushed close together, and the model can then share statistical strength between them — something one-hot (all levels equidistant) and ordinal (a single fake axis) cannot do. Guo & Berkhahn's headline result made this vivid: training only to predict store sales in the Rossmann competition, the learned embedding of the German state variable, projected to 2-D, recovered the geographic map of Germany — nobody told it where the states were. Two lessons, and hold both. First, the methodology lesson, live: with an undertrained one-hot MLP (0.728) the embedding looks like a +0.07 winner — a headline you will see in careless blog posts. Make the baseline fair and the "win" evaporates: 0.774 vs 0.798, a tie inside overlapping error bands (exactly the L023 / L030 discipline — a gap this small with these bars is not a difference). If you ever find yourself excited that embeddings beat one-hot by a mile on a small table, suspect your baseline first.

Second, the representation lesson. Entity embeddings did not unlock new accuracy on credit_g — they land in the same ~0.78 band as one-hot, target encoding, the tuned GBDT (L030), and everything else Q3 measured. This is not a failure of embeddings; it is a statement about the data. On one small, already-flat table there is little similarity structure to learn and little cardinality to compress, so a learned representation has nothing to bite on. Its advantages — compactness at high cardinality, a similarity geometry, and (crucially) a table you can reuse — only pay off when there is structure and scale to exploit.

Explain it back

This is the load-bearing idea — put it in your own words before moving on.

Thesis bridge. Entity embeddings are the hinge on which Q4 turns from "modelling a table" to "learning a representation." Three consequences. (1) Target encoding was an embedding all along. A 1-D learned number per level is the degenerate case; entity embeddings are the d-dimensional generalisation — so the whole Q3 warning about label leakage (L022) rides along, and learned representations must be fit with the same out-of-fold discipline. (2) The payoff is representational, not on this table. Embeddings tie one-hot on credit_g precisely because a single flat table has little similarity or cardinality to exploit — which is the thesis restated: the value the single-table paradigm misses is structural, and you only see it when there is structure. (3) Embeddings are the atom of RDL. The highest-cardinality categoricals are the foreign keys — the ids that point into other tables. One-hot cannot touch them; target encoding leaks on them; but a learned embedding of an entity id, trained end-to-end, is exactly how a relational model represents a customer or a product and lets a graph neural network pass messages between them. Today you learned to embed a column; RDL embeds an entity and its neighbours. Same idea, one table over.

Where this is subtle (know this too)

  • Trees don't need embeddings; nets do. Modern GBDTs (LightGBM, CatBoost) have native categorical handling — CatBoost even uses an ordered, leak-safe target statistic internally. Embeddings are the neural answer to categoricals; part of why trees stay competitive on tabular data is that they never needed one.
  • Target encoding and embeddings are cousins, not rivals. Both learn a numeric representation of a level from the data. Target encoding learns one number in closed form (a mean); an embedding learns several by gradient descent. The leakage discipline is identical.
  • Embeddings can overfit small/rare levels. A level seen twice cannot learn a trustworthy 50-D vector any more than it can a trustworthy mean. Regularisation, smaller d, and dropout matter — and rare levels still need a fallback (an "unknown" bucket).
  • New/unseen levels need a plan. One-hot, target, and embeddings all must decide what to do with a level absent at training time (an all-zero one-hot, the global prior, or a dedicated "unknown" embedding). Silent failure here is a classic production bug.
  • The bake-off ties are on one dataset. As always (L024), read these as a single-dataset methodology demonstration, not a universal ranking. The transferable takeaways are the mechanisms — false order, leakage, learned similarity — not the third-decimal numbers.

Primary reading (today)

The core paper: Cheng Guo & Felix Berkhahn (2016), Entity Embeddings of Categorical Variables — arXiv:1604.06737. Short and readable. Focus on §2–3 (how the embedding layer maps each level to a learned vector, and how it is trained jointly with the network) and §4 (the Rossmann results, including the German-state embedding that recovered geography). Note their central claim: embeddings help most on high-cardinality categoricals and let the learned structure be reused. Background you can skim: Micci-Barreca (2001) for the smoothed target-encoding formula, and the fast.ai tabular docs for the min(50, (card+1)//2) embedding-size heuristic.
