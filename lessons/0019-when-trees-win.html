When Trees Win

Lesson 019 · Phase 1 · ~35 min · preview of Grinsztajn et al. 2022

All of Q2 has been about how tree ensembles work — splits, bagging, boosting, XGBoost/LightGBM/CatBoost, tuning, and stacking. This lesson asks the why: on typical single-table data, a competently-built gradient-boosted tree still matches or beats deep learning — even in 2024–2026. This lesson's single skill: name and recognize the three inductive biases that make trees win — irregular targets, uninformative features, and orientation — and know the honest conditions under which each advantage flips. It is a preview of Grinsztajn, Oyallon & Varoquaux (2022); the full benchmark reproduction comes in Y1 Q3.

One sentence. Trees win on tabular data not because they are "more powerful" but because their inductive biases happen to match tabular data: they can carve irregular boundaries, they ignore uninformative features, and they respect the orientation of individually-meaningful columns — three things a plain neural net does badly.

What "inductive bias" means here

Every learner has an inductive bias: the kind of function it reaches for first, before the data forces it elsewhere. A linear model is biased toward straight boundaries; a neural net is biased toward smooth ones; a decision tree is biased toward axis-aligned, piecewise-constant ones. No bias is universally right — the question is whether a learner's bias matches the data it is given. Grinsztajn et al. benchmarked deep nets against XGBoost and Random Forests across 45 datasets and a 20 000-GPU-hour hyperparameter search, then ran controlled experiments to find why the trees kept winning on medium-sized (~10K-row) tabular data. They distilled it into three challenges for anyone building a tabular neural net — the mirror image of which is the three tree strengths below.

Strength 1 — trees fit irregular (non-smooth) targets

Neural nets are biased toward smooth functions of their inputs: small changes in x give small changes in the prediction. Many tabular targets are not smooth — the risk of default can jump at an income threshold, a fee kicks in at exactly 30 days late. A decision tree is happy to place a hard step anywhere; a smooth-biased net rounds those steps off. The rougher the target, the more the net falls behind.

The widget below makes this concrete with a checkerboard target (the class flips between neighbouring tiles). The tree board is drawn sharp — it can carve every cell with axis-aligned splits. The neural-net board is the same checkerboard through a fixed blur, standing in for the net's smoothness bias. Coarse tiles: both fine. Push to an 8×8 board and the blur (and the accuracy) collapses.

Those readouts are real — a Random Forest vs a 2×256 MLP on synthetic checkerboards (details in the lab). At 2×2 both are near-perfect; by 8×8 the tree holds 0.969 while the MLP falls to 0.837, and at 16×16 the MLP is essentially guessing (0.503). Grinsztajn's version of this experiment smooths the target and shows the tree's advantage shrinks — proving the edge lives precisely in the non-smooth regime.

Strength 2 — trees are robust to uninformative features

Real tables are full of columns that carry little or no signal. A tree does implicit feature selection: at each split it picks the single most informative feature and simply never splits on the junk. A standard MLP has no such filter — every input feeds every first-layer neuron, so uninformative columns inject noise the network must learn to suppress, and it does so imperfectly. Add enough junk and the net degrades faster than the tree.

Read the first row carefully, because it is the honest half of the story: with no junk, the MLP actually wins (0.965 vs 0.936). Neural nets are not weak learners. But as the uninformative features pile up, the tree's built-in selection pays off — the gap swings from −0.029 to +0.049, the MLP shedding 0.139 of accuracy while the tree sheds only 0.060. Grinsztajn shows the same effect both ways: removing uninformative features closes the tree–net gap, and adding them widens it.

Strength 3 — trees preserve the orientation of the data

This is the deepest and least obvious of the three. In a table, each column means something on its own — "age", "balance", "days late". A decision tree splits on one feature at a time, so it is not rotationally invariant: it treats the individual axes as special, which is exactly right when the axes are meaningful. A plain MLP is (approximately) rotationally invariant — rotate the feature space by mixing columns into arbitrary linear combinations and the MLP learns the same thing. That sounds like a virtue until you realize it means the net cannot tell a meaningful column from a scrambled mixture, so it throws away the structure that made the columns informative.

Switch the widget above to Rotate the board. Axis-aligned, the tree's two splits nail the XOR boundary (0.998); the MLP is close behind (0.979). Now hit Rotated: applying a random rotation turns the boundary diagonal. The tree can only approximate a diagonal with a staircase of axis-aligned steps, so it collapses to 0.831 — while the rotation-invariant MLP is unmoved at 0.976. The tree's whole edge was the orientation; destroy it and the edge is gone. Grinsztajn's random-rotation experiment is the sharpest single piece of evidence in the paper: it isolates orientation as a real, exploitable property of tabular data.

The point is not "trees beat nets" — row 2 shows a net winning cleanly. The point is that typical tabular data has all three properties at once (irregular targets, many junk columns, meaningful orientation), and trees are built to exploit exactly those, which is why a tuned GBDT is such a stubborn baseline.

Thesis bridge. This is the intellectual core of Year 1's exit criterion. The RDL undervaluation bet is not "deep learning is bad at tables" — it is "the single-table representation throws away relational structure." Grinsztajn tells us why the flat-table incumbent is so strong (its biases fit flat data), which sets the bar honestly: to argue relational models are undervalued, you must beat a learner whose biases are genuinely well-matched to tabular data — not strawman it. Q3 (lec 024–027) reproduces these experiments on the real 45-dataset benchmark; the whole rest of the curriculum is about finding where structure the flat table destroyed lets a relational model clear this bar.

Where this flips (know this too)

  • Clean, smooth, low-dimensional data. Row 2 above: with no junk features the MLP won. When the target is smooth and every feature matters, the net's biases are fine.
  • Large data. Grinsztajn's result is strongest at ~10K rows; with enough data a net can learn to overcome all three biases. The tree edge is a small-and-medium-data phenomenon.
  • Perceptual / high-cardinality inputs. Images, text, audio, embeddings — smoothness and rotation-invariance are assets there. This whole lesson is about tabular data.
  • Modern tabular nets close the gap. FT-Transformer, RealMLP, TabM and TabPFN (all Year 2) were designed specifically to fix these three biases; on many datasets they now tie tuned GBDTs. "Trees always win" is already too strong — "trees are the honest default baseline" is right.
  • This is a preview. The numbers here are synthetic illustrations. The rigorous claim needs the real benchmark and protocol — that is Q3, and it is what the Q2 checkpoint (L020) tests you can run.
