Inductive Bias: Smoothness

Lesson 025 · Phase 1 · Q3 · ~35 min · core paper: Grinsztajn, Oyallon & Varoquaux 2022, §5.2

Lesson 024 built the measuring instrument: a curated dataset suite and a budget-curve protocol that let Grinsztajn say, defensibly, that tree ensembles beat neural nets on typical tabular data. But a benchmark only tells you that trees win. The paper's real contribution is why — three inductive biases you previewed in L019. This lesson opens the mechanism arc with the first and most fundamental of them: smoothness.

Its single skill: explain the smoothness bias — why an MLP is pulled toward smooth (low-frequency) functions while a tree fits irregular, jagged targets — and know the experiment that proves it is the mechanism: smoothing the target function closes the tree-vs-neural-net gap.

One sentence. Neural networks have a built-in preference for smooth functions, so when a tabular target is irregular (which real ones usually are), the MLP over-smooths it and loses; a tree, being piecewise-constant, can follow the jags — and if you artificially smooth the target, the tree's advantage evaporates.

Warm up — recall before you read

Three questions from earlier lessons, pulled from memory before today's material. A wrong guess here is useful — retrieval (even failed) encodes far better than re-reading.

What "inductive bias" means

Every learner that generalizes must prefer some functions over others — otherwise it could never choose between the infinitely many curves that fit the training points equally well. That built-in preference is its inductive bias. It is not a bug; it is the thing that makes learning possible. The question is only whether a model's bias matches the data.

An MLP's bias is toward smoothness. This has a precise, well-studied form: neural networks trained by gradient descent fit the low-frequency parts of a function first and the high-frequency parts only slowly (or never) — the spectral bias of Rahaman et al. 2019. Intuitively, a network built from smooth activations, initialized near a smooth function and nudged in small steps, has to work hard to produce a sharp corner. A decision tree has the opposite disposition: it carves the input space into axis-aligned boxes and predicts a constant in each, so a single split can produce an arbitrarily sharp step. Its natural output is piecewise-constant and jagged.

See it: who can fit a jagged target? (§5.2)

Here is one irregular 1-D target (grey): a slow trend, a fast wiggle, and a step. A depth-limited tree (green) approximates it with a staircase that hugs the jags; an MLP (blue, dashed) draws a smooth curve that cuts every corner. Now drag the smoothing slider — it Gaussian-smooths the target itself, erasing its high-frequency structure — and watch the error numbers.

On the raw target the MLP's error is over 5× the tree's (MLP MSE 0.070 vs tree 0.013): it simply cannot render the wiggle and the step. Smooth the target a little and the ratio falls to ~2.4×; smooth it fully and the tree's staircase becomes the rougher approximation — the MLP's error drops to 0.19× the tree's and the smooth model wins. The lesson is not "MLPs are bad"; it is that the MLP's bias is a liability exactly when the target is irregular, and an asset when it is smooth. Typical tabular targets are the former.

Predict first

That was one dimension. Grinsztajn ran the real experiment on their full suite: take an irregular multi-dimensional target, Gaussian-smooth it at a growing length-scale, retrain a GBT and an MLP on the smoothed target each time, and plot test accuracy. Commit a guess before you read the curve.

See it: smoothing the target closes the gap

We reproduced it on an irregular 8-feature regression (mean of 5 seeds; test R² for a HistGradientBoosting GBT vs an MLP). At h = 0 — the raw, jagged target — the GBT scores 0.94 and the MLP 0.72: a +0.22 gap. Drag the length-scale up and the gap collapses.

The advantage does not fade gradually with h — it survives while the target still carries its high-frequency structure (91% of the variance at h = 0.5, gap still +0.23), then collapses precisely as that structure is smoothed away (19% variance left at h = 1.0, gap gone). That is the signature of a mechanism, not a coincidence: the tree's whole edge was the part of the target an MLP is biased against. Remove the jags and the MLP even edges ahead — its smoothness bias now matches the (now-smooth) target.

Explain it back

This is the load-bearing idea — put it in your own words before moving on.

Thesis bridge. The smoothness bias is the deepest reason tuned MLPs lose to trees on flat tables — and it is a fact about the target, not just the model. This cuts two ways for the relational thesis. First, it warns you: any relational deep model that ends in an MLP head over a flattened row inherits this bias, so a naïve RDL model can lose to a GBT for exactly this reason — the honest baseline is real. Second, it points at the opening. The irregularity that trips an MLP on a single flat table often comes from relational structure that was crushed into columns — a "days since last order" feature is a jagged function of an entity's event history. A model that reads the history as structure (a graph, a set of events) instead of a pre-smoothed aggregate may represent that irregularity natively rather than fighting its own smoothness bias. Smoothness is both the bar to clear and a hint about where the flattening hurt.

Where this is subtle (know this too)

  • The bias is not absolute. A big enough MLP, trained long enough, can represent sharp functions — the spectral bias is about what it learns easily and first, not what it can represent in principle. On finite data with early stopping, "easily and first" is what you actually get.
  • Smoothness is a property of the target, not of the label type. Classification decision boundaries can be just as jagged as regression surfaces; the argument is about the function the model must fit, not whether y is continuous.
  • Smoothing the target is a diagnostic, not a fix. You would never smooth your real target in production (it destroys signal — watch the R² of both models fall at very large h). It is an ablation: deliberately delete the irregular structure to prove that is what the tree was using.
  • This is one of three biases. Smoothness alone does not explain tree dominance — rotation (L026) and uninformative features (L027) are the other two, and they compound. Do not over-attribute.
  • Preprocessing interacts with it. Quantile/rank transforms and target-aware encodings can regularize a target enough to narrow the gap — a hint of why Year 2's strong-default MLPs (RealMLP, TabM) close so much of it without abandoning the neural approach.
