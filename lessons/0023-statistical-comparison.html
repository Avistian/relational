Statistical Comparison of Classifiers

Lesson 023 · Phase 1 · Q3 · ~40 min · core paper: Demšar 2006

Q3 is about making every clause of the fair-comparison contract rigorous. L021 fixed the split; L022 fixed the features. This lesson fixes the last clause — report the gap honestly — by asking the question every bake-off table begs and almost none answers: is model A's edge over model B real, or is it noise? Its single skill: given per-fold scores for two models, run a paired significance test to decide whether the gap is real — and recognize the failure mode: a naive paired t-test on overlapping CV folds fabricates significance, so use the corrected resampled test on one dataset, and Demšar's rank tests across many.

One sentence. The number you report is a random variable — it depends on which rows landed in which fold — so a mean-accuracy difference is meaningless without a test of whether it could have arisen by chance, and the obvious test (a paired t-test over CV folds) is anticonservative because the folds share training data.

The gap you report is a random variable

You tune two models, cross-validate both, and model A wins by 0.4% accuracy. Ship A? Not yet. Re-draw the folds with a different seed and the gap might vanish, or flip. The cross-validated score is an estimate of generalization error, and estimates have variance: they wobble with the particular train/test partition you happened to draw. A responsible comparison reports not just the mean gap but whether that gap is distinguishable from the wobble. That is a hypothesis test — the null hypothesis being "the two models are equally good, and the observed difference is chance."

Because both models are scored on the same folds, the right family is a paired test: fold by fold, compute the difference di = scoreA(fold i) − scoreB(fold i), and ask whether the mean of those differences is far enough from zero. Pairing cancels the biggest nuisance — some folds are just harder than others — so it is far more powerful than comparing two independent columns of scores.

Predict first

A naive paired t-test on those 100 fold differences returns p ≈ 1.2 × 10−5 — wildly "significant." Commit a guess before you flip the widget to the corrected test.

Why the naive t-test lies: the folds are not independent

A t-test on n numbers assumes they are independent samples, so the standard error of their mean is σ/√n — it shrinks fast as n grows. With 100 folds that denominator is tiny, so even a microscopic mean looks many standard errors from zero. But k-fold CV folds are not independent: any two training folds overlap heavily (in 10-fold CV, two training sets share about 8/9 of their rows). Overlapping training sets produce positively correlated scores, and positive correlation means the true variance of the mean is much larger than σ²/n. The naive test therefore underestimates the variance and rejects the null far too often — it is anticonservative.

Nadeau & Bengio (2003) worked out the correction. Their corrected resampled t-test keeps the same mean and the same sample variance σ² of the differences, but inflates the variance of the mean by a factor that accounts for the train/test overlap:

t = mean(d) / √[ ( 1/n + ntest/ntrain ) · σ²(d) ]    (for k-fold, ntest/ntrain = 1/(k−1))

The extra ntest/ntrain term never goes away as you add folds or repeats, so it caps how confident repeated CV can make you. On our data it turns the verdict inside out: the corrected p is 0.19 — the +0.0098 gap is within noise. Reversing your model choice on a p-hacked 0.4% would be building on sand.

The correction does not make everything "not significant" — a genuinely large, consistent gap (LR beats a stumpy tree by 11 points) stays significant under both tests. The correction simply removes the fake wins that come from treating 100 correlated folds as 100 independent measurements.

Trap: Wilcoxon on CV folds does not fix this. The distribution-free Wilcoxon signed-rank test also assumes its samples are independent, so run on the same 100 correlated folds it also over-rejects (here p ≈ 5e−5, "significant"). Wilcoxon and Friedman are the right tools for comparing over independent datasets (next section), not over the correlated folds of one dataset. For a single dataset, the fix is the variance correction above — not swapping in a different test on the same dependent numbers.

Many models over many datasets: Demšar's diagram

A single dataset can only ever tell you so much. The question a benchmark really asks is: across many datasets, which methods are better? Here you cannot average accuracies (an 80% on one dataset and 80% on another are not commensurable) and you cannot assume normality. Demšar (2006) — the paper that standardized ML comparisons — recommends non-parametric, rank-based tests instead:

  • Rank the k models on each dataset (1 = best), then average each model's rank down the N datasets. Ranks are commensurable where raw scores are not.
  • The Friedman test (a non-parametric repeated-measures ANOVA) asks whether the average ranks differ more than chance allows. If it rejects the null, proceed to a post-hoc test.
  • The Nemenyi post-hoc (all-pairs) says two models differ significantly only if their average ranks differ by at least the critical difference:
CD = qα · √[ k(k+1) / (6N) ]

where k is the number of models and N the number of datasets. Demšar's contribution is to draw this as a CD diagram: an axis of average ranks with a bar joining any group of models that are not significantly different. Below is the verified result of ranking four models over 12 synthetic datasets (the lab builds it). Click a model to see which others it cannot be distinguished from.

Friedman rejects the null (p ≈ 4e−6): the ranks really do differ. With k = 4, N = 12, the critical difference is 1.354 rank units. Logistic regression (mean rank 1.08) and Naive Bayes (2.08) are only 1.0 apart — joined, so not significantly different — but LR beats Random Forest and HistGBDT (both 3.42) by 2.33, comfortably past the CD. (These are near-linear synthetic datasets, so a linear model deservedly tops them; the point is the method, not "trees lose" — you saw in L019 when the ranking flips.)

Which test, when?

SituationRight testWhy
Two models, one dataset, k-fold CVCorrected resampled t-testFolds share training data → correct the variance (Nadeau & Bengio); a naive t-test or Wilcoxon over-rejects.
Two models, one dataset, you can afford it5×2cv paired t-testDietterich 1998: five 2-fold repeats keep train/test disjoint within each repeat, taming the correlation.
Two models, many independent datasetsWilcoxon signed-rankDatasets are genuinely independent and scores are incommensurable → a distribution-free paired test on ranks (Demšar).
Many models, many datasetsFriedman + Nemenyi (CD diagram)Omnibus rank test, then a critical-difference post-hoc; the standard way to read a benchmark leaderboard (Demšar).
Two classifiers, one large test set, no CVMcNemar's testCompares the disagreement cells on a single held-out set (Dietterich 1998) when retraining is too costly.
Thesis bridge. This lesson is the last clause of the honest bar. When a relational model beats a tuned GBDT by a couple of tenths of a point on one RelBench task, the correct reaction is not "we win" — it is a corrected resampled test (or a rank test across tasks) to ask whether the gap survives the wobble. TabReD and TabArena already report results this way, and RelBench spans many tasks precisely so that a Friedman/CD-style verdict is possible. Claim C3 (the RDL gain is real and fair) is not won by a bigger mean; it is won by a gap that a skeptic's significance test cannot dissolve. A result reported as a mean with no test is exactly the kind of finding Kapoor & Narayanan (L022) found evaporating across 17 fields.

Where this is subtle (know this too)

  • Significant ≠ important. With enough data a 0.05% gap can be "significant" and still not worth the extra complexity, latency, or risk. Always pair a p-value with an effect size and a practical threshold.
  • Not significant ≠ equal. Failing to reject the null means you lack evidence of a difference, not proof the models are identical — often it means you need more datasets, not a verdict.
  • Multiple comparisons inflate false positives. Test 20 models pairwise at α = 0.05 and you expect one "win" by chance. Nemenyi/Bonferroni-Dunn control this; ad-hoc all-pairs t-tests do not.
  • The correction has assumptions too. The ntest/ntrain term is an approximation of the true fold correlation; it is far better than ignoring it, but it is not exact. 5×2cv sidesteps it by construction.
  • This is a controlled demo. The synthetic data isolates the mechanism so the p-values are reproducible; the rigorous case for the recommendations is Demšar's paper across real UCI benchmarks (your primary reading).
