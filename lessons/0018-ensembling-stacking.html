Ensembling & Stacking

Lesson 018 · Phase 1 · ~40 min

You have finished the booster tour — XGBoost, LightGBM, CatBoost — plus a Random Forest and a logistic baseline, and in L017 you learned to tune any of them fairly. Natural next question: instead of shipping the single best model, can you combine them into something better? This lesson's single skill: build a stacked ensemble whose meta-learner is trained on out-of-fold base predictions, understand when combining actually helps (diversity), and never leak (in-sample predictions crown the cheater) — Wolpert's 1992 stacked generalization.

One sentence. Averaging or learning a weighted combination of several diverse, individually-good models cancels their uncorrelated errors and can beat the best single model — but the combiner must be trained on predictions each base model made on data it did not see (out-of-fold), or it will simply learn to trust whichever model memorized the training set.

Three ways to combine models

All ensembling answers the same question — "given several models' outputs for a row, what's my final prediction?" — with increasing sophistication:

  • Averaging / blending (voting). Take the mean of the base models' predicted probabilities (or a hard majority vote). Zero extra parameters; the classic Kaggle "average my submissions" trick. sklearn's VotingClassifier.
  • Weighted blend. A convex combination Σ wᵢ pᵢ with hand- or grid-chosen weights. Better if some models are clearly stronger, but the weights are easy to overfit.
  • Stacking (stacked generalization). Treat the base models' predictions as features and train a small meta-learner (level-1 model, e.g. logistic regression) to combine them. It learns the weights — and non-linear interactions — from data. This is Wolpert 1992, and it's what wins leaderboards.

Why combining can beat the best single model: diversity

The intuition is the same variance argument as bagging, but now across model families. If two models make independent errors, averaging them halves the error variance; if they make the same errors, averaging changes nothing. So the fuel for ensembling is diversity: base models that are each accurate but wrong in different places. A booster and a linear model disagree far more than two gradient boosters do.

Stacking: the level-0 / level-1 picture

Stacking has two layers. Level-0 is your pool of base models. Level-1 is a simple meta-learner whose input row is "the base models' predictions for this example" and whose output is the final probability. Train it and you get learned, data-driven blend weights for free.

The whole difficulty is how you generate the level-0 predictions used to train the meta-learner. You cannot ask a base model to predict rows it was trained on — those predictions are memorized, not forecasts. The fix is out-of-fold (OOF) prediction: split the training data into K folds; for each fold, train the base model on the other K−1 folds and predict the held-out fold. Stitch the held-out predictions together and every row now has a prediction from a model that never saw it. Step through it:

Don't fool yourself: the in-sample leak

What happens if you skip the folds and just let each base model predict its own training rows? The In-sample (leak) toggle above shows it: every meta-feature becomes a memorized copy of the label. A model that overfits hardest looks best to the meta-learner, so the meta-learner hands it the most weight — then that trust evaporates on new data. We proved it by dropping a pure memorizer (a 1-nearest-neighbour classifier, which is perfect in-sample because each row's nearest neighbour is itself) into the pool and fitting the meta-learner both ways on a 70/30 split of credit_g:

The 1-NN scores a flawless 1.000 PR-AUC in-sample and a useless 0.704 out-of-fold — so the naïve meta-learner gives it the biggest coefficient (3.00) and its own training score inflates to a mirage 1.000, while its honest held-out score drops to 0.895. The OOF meta-learner sees the 1-NN's true (weak) OOF signal, assigns it a negative weight, and lands at 0.930 — a +0.035 held-out gap purely from not leaking. This is the L004/L005 leakage discipline, now one level up: fit the combiner on out-of-fold predictions only.

Read this honestly, because it is the point. On a 1 000-row single table where the base models are already strong and mostly correlated, ensembling buys almost nothing: the stack reaches 0.902, edging the best single model (RF 0.901) by a hair, and the naïve blend actually trails it (0.899) because it drags four models down toward the weaker logistic. Stacking only the three near-redundant GBDTs gives 0.899 — you cannot ensemble your way past their shared blind spots. The durable takeaways here are not the headline number; they are the mechanism (OOF), the diversity requirement, and the leak. Ensembling pays off when you have (a) genuinely diverse base models and (b) enough data that the meta-learner can learn stable weights — which is exactly the regime of the tabular leaderboards below.

Running it: StackingClassifier

scikit-learn does the OOF bookkeeping for you. Hand StackingClassifier a list of base estimators, a final_estimator (the meta-learner), and cv=5; it generates the level-0 features by internal cross-validation, so you never touch a leaked prediction: 

StackingClassifier(
    estimators=[("cat", CatBoostClassifier(...)),
                ("xgb", XGBClassifier(...)),
                ("logit", make_pipeline(StandardScaler(), LogisticRegression()))],
    final_estimator=LogisticRegression(),
    cv=5, stack_method="predict_proba")

Two rules: keep the meta-learner simple (logistic regression / ridge — a complex meta-learner re-overfits the base predictions), and evaluate the whole stack with an outer CV or held-out set, exactly as in L017 — the stack is a model like any other, so its honest score comes from data the entire fitting procedure never saw. For plain averaging use VotingClassifier(voting="soft").

Thesis bridge. This is the baseline the undervaluation thesis actually has to beat. Modern tabular leaderboards — TabArena, and every serious Kaggle tabular result — are won not by a single tuned booster but by a leak-free stacked ensemble of tuned GBDTs, RealMLP/TabM, and (for small n) TabPFN-style models. So "our relational model beats XGBoost" is the wrong bar; the real bar is "it beats a tuned, stacked ensemble of the best single-table models, under the same CV protocol." Ensembling is where the single-table paradigm is strongest — which is precisely why a relational model that beats it would be such a legible win. And note the continuity with L017: the ensemble, like every base model, must be scored on data its fitting never saw.

Where this breaks (know this too)

  • Redundant base models. Stacking five correlated GBDTs barely beats one of them (our 0.899). No diversity, no gain — add a different family, not another booster.
  • In-sample meta-features. The single most common stacking bug: train the meta-learner on predictions the base models made on their own training rows. It crowns the memorizer and collapses on new data (our +0.035 gap). Always OOF.
  • Complex meta-learner. A deep tree or another booster as the combiner re-overfits the K base-prediction columns. Keep level-1 linear.
  • Tiny data. With few rows the meta-learner cannot estimate stable weights and the OOF predictions are noisy — the ensemble edge shrinks toward zero (our credit_g +0.001).
  • Cost. A stack trains every base model K times inside every outer fold. For a fair, honest number that is a lot of fits — budget for it (L017's fairness point again).

Primary reading (today)

Wolpert (1992), Stacked Generalization, Neural Networks 5(2):241–259 — skim §1–3 for the core idea: a level-1 generalizer trained on the level-0 generalizers' guesses about data held out from them (the "winner-take-all" cross-validation is the OOF construction the widget shows). The scikit-learn stacking guide (and the voting section) is the practical reference for StackingClassifier and VotingClassifier.
