Label Leakage Patterns

Lesson 022 · Phase 1 · Q3 · ~40 min · Kapoor & Narayanan 2022

Lesson 021 fixed one leak at the split level: a random split on time-ordered data lets the future bleed into training. But leakage is a whole family of bugs, and only some live in the split. This lesson zooms out and gives you the map: Kapoor & Narayanan's taxonomy of eight ways label information sneaks from test into train, and the single skill of spotting which leak a feature-engineering choice is committing before it inflates your reported score. Then we reproduce their most unsettling finding — that a leak can make a complex model look like it crushes a simple one when it does not.

One sentence. Data leakage is any way the model learns something at training time it could not legitimately know at prediction time; it is the single most common cause of results that look great in a notebook and collapse in the world — and it has a small, nameable taxonomy you can audit against.

Leakage is not one bug — it is a family

You have already fought leakage five times without a name for the whole family: the point-in-time cutoff (L002), grouped and nested CV (L004), fit-per-fold pipelines (L005), out-of-fold target encoding (L009), and temporal splits (L021). Kapoor & Narayanan (2022) surveyed ML-based science and found leakage in 17 fields, affecting 329 papers — "from textbook errors to open research problems." Their contribution is a fine-grained taxonomy of eight leak types in three families. Learning the map is what turns leakage from a vague fear into a checklist you can run.

Click each type below. Notice how many you have already met — the map mostly names reflexes you have been building, and adds the few you have not.

The three families answer three different questions. Family 1: did test rows leak into training? (no test set, preprocessing/selection on all data, duplicates). Family 2: is a feature legitimate? (a column that proxies the label or would not exist at prediction time). Family 3: is the test set the population you actually care about? (temporal leakage, non-independence, sampling bias). Most of your Q1–Q2 discipline lives in Family 1; Lesson 021 was Family 3 (temporal); today's new sharp edge is Family 2 — illegitimate features, the one that hides in feature engineering.

The dangerous one: an illegitimate feature

A feature is illegitimate (type L2) when its value would not be knowable at the moment you actually make the prediction — most often because it is computed from, or after, the outcome. It is dangerous because it does not look like a leak: no split is shuffled, no scaler is misfit, the pipeline is clean. The column is just… very predictive. That is exactly what a proxy for the label looks like.

Classic examples: a discharge_disposition field that is only set after a patient dies; a closed_reason populated when a support ticket resolves; an account's total_lifetime_value used to predict churn (it already includes the post-churn period). Each hands the model a peek at the answer. The fix is not statistical — it is provenance: for every feature, ask "could this value have been computed strictly before the label event?" A column that fails that test must go, however predictive it is.

Why it is so destructive: predict first

Kapoor & Narayanan ran a reproduction study on civil-war prediction, a field where complex ML models were believed to "vastly outperform" decades-old logistic regression. Every paper claiming the complex-model win failed to reproduce once the leakage was removed. Before you see our synthetic reproduction of that effect, commit a guess.

In the lab you build a dataset with a weak-but-honest signal (both a Random Forest and logistic regression land near 0.72) and then bolt on one illegitimate column — a proxy for the label that a tree can exploit but a linear model cannot. Toggle the leak below.

With the leak in, the Random Forest reads 0.935 and logistic regression 0.719: a +0.217 gap that reads like "complex ML beats the old model by 22 points." Remove the single leaked column and the story evaporates — RF 0.712, LR 0.721, a −0.009 gap. The apparent win was the leak, not the model. This is the collapse in miniature: a leak does not just inflate one number, it can manufacture an entire false conclusion about which methods are better. (Why does the leak favour the tree? The proxy encodes the label non-monotonically — a tree carves it out with two splits, while a linear model sees no usable direction. A more flexible model exploits a leak harder, which is precisely why "the fancy model won" is a leak-smell.)

Thesis bridge. This is the sharpest BAR the dossier has yet recorded, and it cuts both ways. It raises the bar because it means a headline relational-vs-GBDT win is not evidence until leakage is ruled out — a suspiciously large margin is a leak hypothesis first, a method hypothesis second (exactly the L020 reflex). It also arms the skeptic: "maybe RDL's wins are leakage too." Our answer has to be structural — RelBench enforces strict point-in-time cutoffs by construction (Fey 2024), and every reported gain travels with a leakage audit. The thesis is won with a clean win over a fair baseline, never with a big number.

The second leak the lab measures: non-independence

Family 1's L1.4 (duplicates) and Family 3's L3.2 (non-independence) are close cousins, and the lab shows them together. When the same underlying record appears more than once — a customer logged twice, a near-duplicate sensor reading — a random split can put one copy in train and its twin in test. The model half-memorizes the test answer from its training twin. In the lab: a naïve random 5-fold CV reads 0.948; a GroupKFold keyed on the record id, so no record straddles a fold, reads the honest 0.876 — a +0.072 phantom. This is the same grouped-split fix from L004, now recognized as a named leak type. The lesson: identity is a leakage surface. Always ask what the unit of independence really is.

The defense: a model info sheet

Kapoor & Narayanan's prescription is not "be careful" — it is a model info sheet: a short, structured form you fill out for any ML claim, with a question per leak type, that forces the leak to surface before publication. It is the leakage analogue of a pre-registration. None of the civil-war errors could be caught by reading the papers; every one would have been caught by the sheet. Where this is subtle (know this too)

  • A clean pipeline can still leak. L2 leaks pass every fit-per-fold and grouped-split check. Only provenance auditing catches them — the pipeline discipline from Q1 is necessary, not sufficient.
  • The fancier model is not "better" — it exploited the leak more. A big complex-vs-simple gap is a leak hypothesis first. Under an honest evaluation the gap usually shrinks (echoing Grinsztajn, L019, and TabReD, L021).
  • Leakage ≠ overfitting. Overfitting shows up as a train/test gap you can see; leakage inflates the test score too, so cross-validation looks fine. That is what makes it so dangerous.
  • Sampling bias (L3.3) is the quietest. Your split can be perfectly honest and your number still not generalize, because the test set is not the population your claim is about. Define that population up front.
  • This is a controlled demo. The synthetic collapse isolates the mechanism; the real evidence is 329 papers across 17 fields (your primary reading).
