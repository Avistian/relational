Data Splits in the Wild

Lesson 021 · Phase 1 · Q3 opener · ~40 min · previews TabReD (Rubachev et al. 2024)

Q2 ended with the fair-comparison contract: to compare two models honestly you fix the data, the split, the metric, the tuning budget, and the preprocessing scope up front. Q3 is about making each of those rigorous. This lesson takes the one that quietly decides whether your whole evaluation is real or a mirage: the split. Its single skill: recognize when a random (i.i.d.) split leaks the future and inflates your reported score, and build a temporal split — train on the past, test on the future — that measures what deployment actually faces.

One sentence. A random train/test split assumes the rows are interchangeable; when the data has a time order and the world drifts, that assumption puts the future in your training set, so the number you report is optimistic — a temporal split forbids the peek and tells you the truth.

Every random split makes one assumption

train_test_split(..., shuffle=True) and KFold(shuffle=True) are the reflex of every tabular pipeline, and they carry a hidden premise: the rows are i.i.d. — independent and identically distributed, hence exchangeable. Under that premise, which rows land in test versus train genuinely does not matter, so a random draw is the fair one. That premise is often false in production. Data arrives over time, and the world it measures drifts: prices move, fraud tactics evolve, user tastes shift, a pricing rule changes at a fixed date. Rows from March are not exchangeable with rows from December.

When you deploy a model, it is always trained on the past and asked to predict the future. A random split breaks that arrow of time: it sprinkles test rows uniformly across the whole history, so a test row from December can sit right next to training rows from the same December. The model gets to train on the period it is scored on. That is not the situation it will face in production — and it makes the model look better than it is.

See it: random scatters, temporal cuts once

Below is the same stream of rows, ordered oldest→newest, split two ways. The top strip is a random 20% holdout; the bottom is a temporal one. Watch where the red test rows land. (Reshuffle the random split a few times — the story never changes.)

In the random strip, test rows are salted through every period, so for almost every test row there is a training row from the same week — the model has effectively seen the neighbourhood of the answer. In the temporal strip there is a single cut: everything left of the dashed line is train (the past), everything right is test (the future). The model must extrapolate forward into a period it never touched. Only the second picture matches deployment.

Why a random split flatters your model

There are three distinct ways the random split leaks, and it is worth naming them separately:

  • Concept drift. The relationship between features and label changes over time. A random split lets the model learn the current relationship from test-period training rows; a temporal split forces it to rely on a possibly-stale relationship learned from the past. This is the big one, and the one we visualize next.
  • Temporal autocorrelation / near-duplicates. Consecutive rows are often near-copies (the same sensor two seconds later, the same customer next day). A random split scatters near-duplicate pairs across train and test, so the model can nearly memorize a test row from its train-set twin — a leak even when nothing drifts. (TabReD flags the popular electricity dataset for exactly this.)
  • Point-in-time feature leakage. Features computed over a window that straddles the cutoff encode the future — the same design-matrix leak from L002, now at the split level.

Concept drift, made visible

Here is the drift in our synthetic stream (the lab builds it). The rule that generates the label rotates over time: early rows are labelled mostly by feature x0, late rows mostly by x1, while x2 stays a steady minor signal. The bars are the real correlation of each feature with the label in five equal time buckets; the dial on the right is the direction of the decision rule the model must learn. Drag the slider through time.

The base rate barely moves (prevalence stays ≈ 0.50 in every bucket), so this is pure concept drift, not a shifting class balance — the rule changes, not how many positives there are. corr(x0, y) decays from +0.72 to +0.12 while corr(x1, y) climbs from +0.10 to +0.71. A model that trained only on the early buckets learned to trust x0 — exactly the wrong instinct by the late period. A random split hides this because its training folds contain late-bucket rows too; a temporal split exposes it in full.

How big is the lie? Predict first

We trained a plain logistic regression on this stream two ways: a random 5-fold CV (shuffle=True) and a temporal holdout (train on the first 80% of time, test on the last 20%). Same data, same model, same metric (accuracy). Commit a guess before you see the numbers.

Read the ordering carefully — it is the whole lesson in one row. Random CV (0.846) is the most optimistic: its training folds are contaminated with the deployment period. Temporal holdout (0.758) is the harshest and most honest: it is scored entirely on the most-drifted final period, using only earlier data. Reporting the random number would have over-promised deployment accuracy by ~9 points — the kind of gap that turns a "ship it" into an incident. Notice the gap is smaller for the flexible booster only because it fit the training period a little less confidently here; drift punishes both model classes.

The honest CV: TimeSeriesSplit

A single temporal holdout wastes data and gives one noisy number. The cross-validated version is time-series CV (sklearn's TimeSeriesSplit): an expanding window where every fold trains on a block of the past and validates on the block immediately after it — never the reverse. 

fold 1:  train [■■□□□□]  test [■]        # rows are TIME-ORDERED, never shuffled
fold 2:  train [■■■□□□]  test [■]
fold 3:  train [■■■■□□]  test [■]
fold 4:  train [■■■■■□]  test [■]

It landed at 0.819 — between the optimistic random CV and the single last-block holdout, because its earlier folds test on less-drifted periods while the final holdout tests only on the hardest one. Two rules travel with it: (1) your validation slice for tuning and early stopping must also be temporal (a random validation fold leaks the future into model selection — the same selection-bias trap as L004, now on the time axis); and (2) if the same entity recurs over time (a customer, a device), you may need grouped and temporal splits together, so no entity straddles the cut.

The default reflex should flip: assume temporal until you can argue the rows are truly exchangeable, not the other way around. If a dataset has no timestamp, that is a limitation to note (and TabReD's central complaint about academic benchmarks), not a license to shuffle.

Thesis bridge. Time-respecting evaluation is not a footnote for the relational thesis — it is load-bearing. Relational Deep Learning builds a temporal entity graph and RelBench scores every task with a strict time cutoff (Fey et al. 2024), precisely because relational features (a customer's past orders, a device's event history) are point-in-time by construction. And TabReD (Rubachev et al. 2024) shows on real industrial data that switching from random to time-based splits "leads to significant differences in rankings and relative performance of methods… XGBoost's performance margin diminishes," and that random splits "could present overly optimistic performance estimates." So this is both the split leg of the fair-comparison contract and the reason claim C3 (the RDL gain must survive honest temporal evaluation) is where the thesis is won or lost. A relational win under a random split would prove nothing.

Where this is subtle (know this too)

  • A temporal split is harder and smaller. You train on less data and test on the most-shifted period, so scores drop — that is the point, not a bug. Do not "fix" it by shuffling.
  • Random is not always wrong. For truly static, order-free tables (many UCI classics), a stratified random split is correct and a temporal one is impossible. The skill is knowing which world you are in.
  • Drift ≠ imbalance. Our prevalence was flat; the leak came from the rule moving. Watch for both — a shifting base rate is a second, separate kind of drift.
  • Tune on a temporal validation slice. A perfect test split with a random validation split still leaks the future into hyperparameter choice and early stopping.
  • This is a controlled demo. The synthetic stream isolates concept drift so you can see the mechanism; the rigorous evidence is TabReD on eight real industrial datasets (your primary reading).
